Every article about a Lithuanian parish in America went into the same database — every closure and every merger, every Vatican appeal and every court case, every fundraising campaign, every final Mass, every anniversary and celebration, every demolition. Each one, on its own, read like just another chapter in the life of a single community. By the time I reached the end, eighty-three Lithuanian Catholic parishes had entered the record. Fifty-five of them had closed, merged, or lost their independence.

At first I believed I was documenting history. Somewhere along the way, history began to explain itself — and that is the reason for this Substack.

Although my research began with Lithuanian Catholic parishes, the question it raises belongs to every immigrant community in America: Polish, Slovak, Croatian, Ukrainian, Italian, German, Hungarian, Lebanese, Vietnamese. These were communities whose churches became far more than the place where Mass was celebrated on Sunday. They became schools and archives, scout troops and choirs, museums, language classes, and dance groups — the quiet machinery by which a people handed its culture from one generation to the next. When one of those churches closes, the loss is rarely confined to a building. Something much larger is set loose. The question that has occupied me for months is simply: why.

### We Have Been Reading the Story One Parish at a Time

For nearly two decades, *Draugas* faithfully reported each Lithuanian parish closure as it happened. One year it was Shenandoah; another year Boston, then New York, then Waterbury, then Philadelphia, then the earlier chapters of Detroit’s own parish history. Each article described a different community facing its own particular circumstances, and the explanations had a familiar sound to them. The neighborhood had changed. The congregation had grown older. The building needed expensive repairs. There were fewer priests. Attendance had declined.

Every parish seemed to have its own story, and because we encountered those stories one at a time — a year or more apart, in a community a hundred miles from the last — we naturally assumed that each closure had its own separate cause. That is the limit of reading the news as it arrives. An archive lets you do something the newspaper never could: it lets you lay all of those stories side by side and ask a different question. Not *why did this parish close*, but *what appears when every one of these closures is read together*. That is the moment history stops being a sequence of sad events and begins to reveal a shape that no single article could show.

### The Question That Kept Returning

When I began, I expected the evidence to confirm the assumptions we have repeated for years. I expected to find that financially healthy parishes survived, that thriving communities outlasted struggling ones, that determined parishioners stood a better chance of keeping their churches open. Some of those things mattered at the margins. None of them consistently explained what actually happened.

Instead, I kept returning to a single question — one I had not set out to ask. Who owned the church? Not spiritually, not emotionally, not historically. Legally. Who held the deed. Once I began reading the record through that lens, stories that had seemed entirely unrelated began to fit together, as if they had been waiting for the right question all along.

### Money Was Not Enough

The strongest assumption surrounding parish closures is that financial health determines survival. The record suggests otherwise, and it does so again and again.

St. Casimir Parish in Worcester, Massachusetts, had completed nearly one million dollars in repairs and carried no debt when it was closed. St. George Parish in Shenandoah had accumulated roughly a million dollars in savings and inspired more than a thousand donors to help preserve one of the oldest Lithuanian churches in America. In Manhattan, the parishioners of Our Lady of Vilnius — better known to us as Gate of Dawn — offered to pay for the roof repairs themselves. None of those churches survived.

The Shenandoah story is especially revealing, because it shows how even a settled financial fact can become contested. The parish hired its own engineer, who estimated the necessary repairs at roughly $360,000. The diocese rejected that estimate and commissioned further engineering reports, and the projected cost climbed into the millions before eventually approaching nine million dollars. Which raises a question that turns out to sit underneath a great many closures: who decides whether a church is repairable? The community could raise the money. The owner determined whether the building would be repaired. That distinction is quiet, and it is decisive.

### Neither Was Loyalty

If money alone could not preserve a parish, perhaps devotion could. No one can read these histories honestly and conclude that Lithuanian communities simply gave up. They fought — with a persistence that is humbling to read about.

In New York, nearly four thousand people signed petitions to save Gate of Dawn. Parishioners took their case to the civil courts and to the Vatican. Historic-preservation advocates joined the effort. Lithuania’s own presidents, Valdas Adamkus and later Gitanas Nausėda, spoke publicly in support of preserving Lithuanian churches, and the 2002 U.S.–Lithuania Cultural Heritage Agreement was invoked on the church’s behalf. In Manhattan, parishioners managed to delay demolition through litigation for nearly four years.

And yet, when New York’s highest court finally ruled in 2011, it did not decide whether the church should remain open. It decided who possessed the legal authority to make that decision — and the answer was not the congregation. The church was sold to a developer and demolished. Reading that case changed how I understood every other one. These communities had extraordinary commitment. What they lacked was authority over the final decision.

### The Pattern Hidden in Plain Sight

The remarkable thing about these fifty-five closures is not that the parishes were all alike. They were not. Some were flourishing and some were struggling. Some carried debt; others held substantial savings. Some had just renovated their buildings; others badly needed to. Some accepted the end with quiet sadness; others fought for years. The bishops were different, the dioceses were different, the decades were different, the circumstances were different.

One feature, however, appeared with remarkable consistency: the parish property belonged to the diocese.

I want to be careful here, because this is not an accusation. It is not a criticism of bishops, who often face genuinely difficult demographic, pastoral, and financial realities, and who are charged with weighing the good of an entire diocese. It is simply a historical observation — and observations are the business of an archive. When the legal authority to decide a parish’s future rests outside the congregation, the range of choices available to that congregation is fundamentally different from what most parishioners ever realize. History suggests we have paid far less attention to that single fact than it deserves.

### The Exceptions That Explain the Rule

Historians often learn as much from the exceptions as from the pattern, and the exceptions here are the most instructive part of the whole story.

Pennsylvania’s anthracite coal region once held thirty-one Lithuanian parishes. Twenty-nine of them were Roman Catholic, and today every one of those twenty-nine has closed. One survives: Scranton’s Divine Providence Lithuanian National Catholic Church. (This is a different parish from the Divine Providence in Southfield, Michigan, at the center of this publication; the two share a name but not a diocese, and Scranton’s belongs to the Lithuanian National Catholic tradition rather than the Roman Catholic one.) Its history followed a different path for a reason that has nothing to do with faith or effort and everything to do with governance — because its ownership followed a different path.

Lawrence, Massachusetts, offers the comparison in its purest form. In 2002, two Lithuanian parishes in the same city reached the end of their histories in the same year. The Roman Catholic parish was closed by decision of the archdiocese. The independent Lithuanian National Catholic parish also closed — but by decision of its own members, who chose to retain the proceeds to support their cemetery. The same city, the same year, the same demographic pressures. Different ownership. Different authority. Two very different kinds of ending.

Other examples point the same direction. In Montreal, where Quebec’s legal framework has historically structured parish property differently than many American dioceses, both Lithuanian parishes survived. Even a Lithuanian Lutheran congregation belongs in this account: Tėviškė, in Chicago’s Marquette Park, purchased its church — a former synagogue — in 1957, and ever since it has made every major decision about its own future. None of these cases offers a universal blueprint, and I do not present them as one. Together, though, they suggest that governance — who holds the deed, and who decides — deserves far more attention than it has received.

### Why This Matters Now

As I write this, Detroit’s Divine Providence Lithuanian Catholic Parish is taking part in the Archdiocese of Detroit’s restructuring process. No closure decree has been issued. No outcome has been decided. And that, precisely, is what matters.

The history I have spent these months reconstructing points to one practical truth above all others: communities have their greatest opportunity to shape their future *before* decisions become irreversible. Once formal closure proceedings begin, the choices available to parishioners narrow dramatically and quickly. History cannot tell Detroit what will happen, and it cannot prescribe a single correct solution. What it can do is tell us which questions deserve to be asked while there is still time to ask them.

What governance models are possible? What legal structures already exist, here and elsewhere? Can the cultural institutions that grew up around a parish — the school, the archive, the choir, the language class — be protected even under difficult circumstances? Can ownership and stewardship be imagined differently while remaining wholly faithful to the Church’s mission? These are not questions of opposition. They are questions of stewardship, and they are best asked early, in a spirit of cooperation, and out loud.

### Why I’m Writing This

This Substack is not about nostalgia, and it is not about assigning blame. It is about understanding the institutions our immigrant communities built.

Over the coming months I will share the stories behind this research: the rise and fall of Lithuanian parishes across America, the court cases that reshaped whole communities, the governance structures that most parishioners never knew existed, the archives that quietly preserve what has been forgotten, and the decisions that determined which churches survived and which did not.

Most of all, I hope these essays begin conversations that reach well beyond the Lithuanian-American community — because every immigrant community eventually arrives at the same question: how do you preserve not simply a building, but the whole world that building made possible?

I began this project believing I was documenting fifty-five separate stories. I finished convinced that I had been reading one. It is not, in the end, a story about decline. It is a story about governance, stewardship, memory, and the responsibility each generation inherits from the one before it — and it begins with a question far older, and far more important, than any single parish:

Who owns the future?
